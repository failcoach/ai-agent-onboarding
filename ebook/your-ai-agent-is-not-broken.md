# Your AI Agent Is Not Broken. You Just Never Onboarded It.

*By Miha Matlievski / Fail Coach*

---

## I Wanted to Throw My Laptop Out the Window

I'm not going to pretend this was easy.

When I built my first AI agent, I spent hours on it. Gave it a role. Told it to "be strategic" and "help me grow my business."

It gave me garbage.

Generic advice. Wrong priorities. Stuff that sounded smart but meant nothing. I tried another framework. Another tool. Another YouTube tutorial from a guy who's never run a real company.

Same result.

Most people quit here. I didn't. Not because I'm smarter. I'm just too stubborn to walk away from something I know should work. So I kept breaking it and rebuilding it, week after week, getting more frustrated each time.

Then one morning, while I was complaining to a friend about how useless AI agents are, he asked me a simple question:

"How long did it take your last employee to become useful?"

I said three months. Maybe four.

He said: "And how long did you give your AI agent?"

An afternoon.

That hit me. I was treating my AI agent like software. Install it, click a button, expect magic. But an AI agent isn't software. It's a new hire. And I was being the worst boss imaginable.

---

## The YouTube Problem

Go watch any YouTube video about building AI agents. You'll see the same thing over and over.

Some guy creates an "AI org chart." He makes a CEO agent, a CMO agent, a CTO agent. Sounds impressive.

Then you look at how he builds them.

"I want my CEO to be like Alex Hormozi. Go research him and build the agent based on that."

That's not how you hire a CEO. That's not how you hire anyone.

Imagine walking into an interview and saying: "I want you to be like Elon Musk. Figure it out." You'd get laughed out of the room.

And then, after building this whole "executive team" of agents, the task he gives his CEO is: "Write me a newsletter about open source LLM models."

A CEO. Writing a newsletter. That's an intern's job.

The whole org chart was theater. Titles without roles. Names without job descriptions. Fancy labels on empty boxes.

This is what happens when people who've never managed anyone try to manage AI. They build what looks impressive instead of what actually works.

## You Already Know How to Do This

Here's the thing about AI agents that nobody talks about.

The skills you need to make an AI agent work are the exact same skills you need to manage a human team. And most business owners are terrible at both.

Be honest with yourself for a second.

Do you have written job descriptions for your team? Real ones, not the ones you copied from a job board three years ago.

Do you have development plans for your people? Do you do regular one-on-ones? Do you give feedback within 24 hours, or do you save it all for some annual review that everyone hates?

Do you know what tools and access your people need on day one?

If you answered no to most of those, that's exactly why your AI agent isn't working. You're bringing the same broken management to a new technology and wondering why it fails.

An AI agent needs everything a human employee needs. It just needs it in a different format.

---

## Give It a Real Job Description

Not "be helpful." Not "act like a CEO." Not "be strategic."

A real job description. Every one of my agents has a written brief that answers five questions:

1. **What exactly does this agent do?** Not a vague role. Specific tasks, specific outputs.
2. **What decisions can it make on its own?** And what needs to come to me first?
3. **What information does it need?** Documents, data, context about my business.
4. **What does success look like?** How do I measure good versus just... text?
5. **What does it NOT do?** Boundaries matter as much as responsibilities.

When I hire a human, I don't say "figure it out." I tell them: here's your role, here's your scope, here are the boundaries, here's how I'll know if you're doing well.

My agents get the same thing. Without this, you're not managing. You're hoping.

---

## Give It the Right Tools

Think about a new employee's first day at your company.

If they show up and don't have a computer, can't access your systems, don't have login credentials, and nobody shows them where the files are, what happens?

Nothing. They sit there. Useless. Not because they're incompetent, but because you failed to set them up.

AI agents are the same. If your agent can't access your data, can't connect to your systems, can't read the documents it needs to read, it can't do its job.

Before you blame the agent, check what you gave it to work with.

---

## Give It a Memory

This is the one most people miss completely.

Imagine if your best employee woke up every morning with total amnesia. No memory of yesterday's conversations. No idea what happened last week. Every single day, starting from zero.

That's what your AI agent is doing if you haven't set up its memory properly.

When I first started, my agents were like that. Every session was groundhog day. I'd explain the same context, give the same background, repeat the same preferences. Over and over.

Now, every one of my agents has structured memory. At the end of each session, everything important gets documented and stored. Next session, the agent picks up where it left off. It remembers decisions, context, preferences, history.

It took me weeks to get this right. The memory system had to evolve. It started messy, got bloated, and I had to restructure it more than once. Just like training an employee to take proper notes and maintain their own files.

But once it clicked, the difference was night and day. My agents went from tools to teammates.

## Manage It Like You'd Manage Your Best Employee

Here's where it all comes together.

Good managers don't just hire people and disappear. They invest in their people. Continuously. They create development plans. They have regular check-ins. They give timely feedback. They adjust expectations based on performance. They help their people grow.

You need to do the same with your AI agent.

After every task, ask yourself: did it deliver what I needed? If not, why? Was the instruction unclear? Was it missing context? Does it need a different approach?

Then tell the agent. Update its instructions. Refine its memory. Adjust its scope.

This is not a one-time setup. This is ongoing management. Weekly, sometimes daily, especially in the beginning.

The people who fail with AI agents are the same people who fail with human employees. They expect results without investing time. They want performance without providing feedback. They set it up once and blame it when things go wrong.

One prompt will never be enough. Just like one conversation will never be enough to make a new hire productive.

---

## The Timeline Is Real

Everybody wants their AI agent working perfectly by tomorrow.

It won't happen. Just like your best employee didn't hit their stride on day one.

Simple agent, focused task, clear data? A few weeks to get it running well.

Complex agent, multiple responsibilities, needs to understand your business? A few months. Minimum.

And it will get better over time. Way better. My agents today are unrecognizable compared to what they were three months ago. They've accumulated context, learned patterns, and their output quality has compounded.

But only because I treated them like employees, not like apps.

Give it time. Invest in the process. The compound returns are worth it.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

If you read this and thought "I don't do most of this for my human team either," then you just discovered something important.

The reason your AI agent isn't working is the same reason your team isn't performing at its best. You don't have a system for developing people. You don't give structured feedback. You don't create development plans. You don't invest in onboarding.

And here's the real kicker: AI didn't create this problem. It just made it impossible to ignore.

When your employee underperforms, you can blame them. Their attitude, their skills, their motivation. When your AI agent underperforms, there's nobody to blame but the person who set it up.

You.

The good news? This is fixable. The skills that make your AI agents work are the same skills that will fix your human team. Build one system, and both sides improve.
